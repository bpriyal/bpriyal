Delta and Gamma: The Geometry of Risk

#1 Where Calculus Meets Hedging — Modeling Sensitivity with Derivatives

Source & Code Reference

Primer Ch. 3.6 (Greeks), Ch. 5 (Taylor Series)
C++ Implementation: greeks.cpp

Model the Problem

We model the change in a call option's price \(C(S,t)\) relative to small movements in the underlying asset \(S\). Delta (\(\Delta\)) is the first derivative: \[ \Delta = \frac{\partial C}{\partial S} \] and Gamma (\(\Gamma\)) is the second derivative: \[ \Gamma = \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial S^2}. \]

Explain the Modelled Events

  • Delta (Δ): Slope of the price curve. Δ=0.5 → $1 increase in stock price increases option value by $0.50.
  • Gamma (Γ): Curvature of the price curve. High gamma implies rapid changes in Δ, making hedging difficult.

Solution in Math

Black-Scholes formula:

\[ C = S N(d_1) - K e^{-rT} N(d_2) \]
  • Delta: \( \Delta = N(d_1) \)
  • Gamma: \( \Gamma = \frac{N'(d_1)}{S\sigma\sqrt{T}} \), where \(N'(x)\) is the standard normal PDF
  • Taylor Approximation: \[ dC \approx \Delta\, dS + \frac{1}{2} \Gamma (dS)^2 \]

Extrapolation (C++ Implementation)

  • Struct Greeks returns std::tuple<Delta, Gamma>
  • Compute d1 and N'(d1) once for multiple Greeks
  • Finite Difference Verification: \[ \Gamma \approx \frac{C(S+h) - 2C(S) + C(S-h)}{h^2} \]
