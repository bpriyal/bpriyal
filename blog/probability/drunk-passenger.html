The Drunk Passenger

Where recursion hides behind intuition

One hundred passengers board a plane with exactly one hundred seats. The first passenger is drunk and chooses a seat uniformly at random. Every subsequent passenger takes their own seat if available; otherwise, they choose randomly from the remaining seats.

What is the probability that the last passenger sits in their assigned seat?

Modeling the Problem

Despite the long boarding process, only two seats matter: Seat 1 and Seat 100. The uncertainty resolves the moment one of these seats is taken by a displaced passenger.

The Key Insight

Whenever a passenger must choose randomly, Seat 1 and Seat 100 are symmetric options. No rule biases one over the other.

Eventually, one of them is chosen first.

Result

$$ P(\text{last passenger sits correctly}) = \frac{1}{2} $$
